The Ukulele: History & Types

A Brief History

The ukulele, often called the "uke," is a small, four-stringed instrument with a cheerful sound and an even brighter history. The ukulele originated in Hawaii during the late 19th century, inspired by small Portuguese instruments like the machete and cavaquinho. Immigrants from Madeira, Portugal, brought these instruments to Hawaii around 1879.

The Hawaiians adapted the instrument, giving it the name "ukulele," which means "jumping flea" â€” possibly inspired by the players' quick finger movements on the fretboard. The ukulele quickly became a staple of Hawaiian music and culture. By the early 20th century, its popularity spread to the mainland United States and beyond, especially during the Jazz Age of the 1920s.

Today, the ukulele is beloved worldwide for its light, tropical sound and easy-to-learn charm!

Types of Ukulele

Ukuleles come in several shapes and sizes, each with its own unique sound and feel. Here are the four main types:

  1. Soprano Ukulele
    • The smallest and most traditional size.
    • Bright, classic "ukulele" sound.
    • Common tuning: G-C-E-A.
  2. Concert Ukulele
    • Slightly larger than the soprano.
    • Louder sound with more depth.
    • Easier for people with bigger hands.
    • Common tuning: G-C-E-A.
  3. Tenor Ukulele
    • Bigger body and longer neck.
    • Rich, warm tone with strong projection.
    • Often used by professional performers.
    • Common tuning: G-C-E-A.
  4. Baritone Ukulele
    • Largest of the ukulele family.
    • Deeper, guitar-like sound.
    • Tuned like the top four strings of a guitar: D-G-B-E.

Fun Fact!

There are also hybrid ukuleles like the banjolele (a mix of a banjo and a ukulele) and electric ukuleles designed for amplified performances.

Ukulele Anatomy & Quiz

Source: Bernadette Teaches Music

This video introduces the essential body parts of the ukulele. Knowing these parts is crucial for learning and discussing the instrument. The ukulele is divided into three main parts: the head (or headstock), the neck, and the body.

On the head, you'll find the tuners that adjust the strings (GCEA) and the nut that holds them in place. The neck features the fingerboard where you place your fingers, along with frets (spaces), fret wires (lines), and fret markers (dots). The body includes the sound hole, often called the ukulele's mouth, and the bridge and saddle. Some ukuleles may also have strap pins or buttons. Understanding these components is presented as a necessary step before moving on to playing. The video also includes a quiz and review to reinforce learning


How to Hold the Ukulele

Source: Bernadette Teaches Music

This video explains the correct way to hold your ukulele, a crucial first step for beginners. It emphasizes positioning the back of the ukulele against your belly or torso with the sound hole facing outwards towards your audience. Facing the sound hole towards yourself is incorrect and causes wrist strain.

Secure the ukulele by using your forearm as a "seatbelt"; the instrument should stay in place even if you briefly release your picking hand. For the fretting hand, place your thumb "thumbs up" on the back of the neck, keeping your hand unfolded and curved. When fretting notes or chords, use the very tip of your finger and apply a squeeze.

For picking, let your thumb "fall" down across the strings, avoiding pulling or tweezing. The video suggests holding the ukulele at an angle similar to 2 o'clock to 2:45. Learning this proper hold is foundational for future lessons.


Strumming for Beginners

Source: Cynthia Lin

This video focuses on teaching you how to achieve a clear and articulated ukulele strum where you can hear every note. It introduces techniques starting with using your thumb to understand the amount of power needed, practicing plucking strings individually and strumming down, stopping on the next string. For better control, you can push your thumb into the fretboard.

The video then moves to using the index finger, often with the thumb for support. You should use the middle of your nail for downstrums and the fleshy tip of your index finger for upstrums. The key is to maintain grip and control over each string. Practicing slowly is essential to build muscle memory and ensure your strum isn't muddy. Mastering this technique is a crucial step for clear playing.


How to Tune Your Ukulele

Source: Bernadette Teaches Music

This video teaches you how to tune your ukulele using a clip-on tuner. Start by attaching the tuner to the ukulele's headstock and turning it on. The video strongly recommends using the Chromatic (C) setting on your tuner, especially when your ukulele is new or the strings are very loose, as they might not be close to the standard tuning. The standard ukulele tuning is GCEA.

To tune, follow each string to its correct tuner peg. Pluck a string and turn the peg until the tuner screen shows the correct note (G, C, E, or A) and indicates it's in tune, usually by turning green. An important step is to gently stretch each string after tuning it, then re-tune, which helps the strings settle and hold their pitch. Developing the habit of tuning your ukulele every time you play is also recommended.


Reading Chord Charts

Source: Ukulele Wales

This video provides a quick guide to reading ukulele chord charts. These charts are diagrams that help you remember how to play a chord. A chord chart represents the fretboard of your ukulele.

The horizontal lines on the chart are the frets, and the vertical lines represent your strings, which are tuned G, C, E, and A. You read the chart as if you are looking at your ukulele turned this way. Dots on the chart show you where to place your fingers. Sometimes these dots contain numbers indicating which finger to use, but not always. For chords like C or F, you look at the dot's position on the grid. A chart with no dots means you just strum the open strings (like A minor 7). A line going across the chart indicates a barre chord, where one finger presses down all strings on a specific fret. Understanding these diagrams is key to learning new chords.

Ukulele Study Guide

Source: MMMM

This resource serves as a study guide focused on labelling a ukulele chord sheet and understanding some basic ukulele concepts. It explains how to read a chord sheet diagram, which represents the ukulele's fretboard. Key elements covered include labelling the open notes (G, C, E, A) and associating them with the strings (4=G, 3=C, 2=E, 1=A). The guide also details how to label the frets (Fret 1, 2 etc.) and how finger numbers are represented on a fretboard diagram using the notation I=1, M=2, R=3, P=4. Understanding these labels is foundational for learning to read and play ukulele chords from charts.

Scales for Ukulele: C and D

Source: TheSumoftheParts.com

This resource provides helpful diagrams for learning several essential ukulele scales in both the keys of C and D. It includes diagrams for the major scale, major pentatonic scale, minor pentatonic scale, and blues scale in both keys.

Each scale is presented using both standard musical notation and ukulele tablature (TAB). The TAB notation clearly shows you which fret to play on each string. For the D scales, it also indicates suggested finger numbers to use. Learning these scales provides a foundation for understanding melody, playing lead lines, and improvising on the ukulele.
