The Piano: History & Development

Early Origins

The piano's story begins in the early 1700s in Italy. Before the piano, musicians played the harpsichord and clavichord; both instruments could not play loud and soft dynamics well.

Key Developments

  1. Bartolomeo Cristofori:
    • The inventor of the piano was an Italian craftsman named Bartolomeo Cristofori around the year 1700. His design allowed players to control the volume of each note by pressing the keys harder or softer. The instrument was first called the pianoforte (meaning 'soft-loud' in Italian).
  2. Development:
    • Over the 18th and 19th centuries, the piano was improved with stronger frames (from wood to cast iron) and more keys, expanding the range from 5 octaves to 7+ octaves.
  3. Golden Age:
    • During the Romantic Era (1800s), composers like Beethoven, Chopin, and Liszt wrote masterpieces for the piano. The piano became the centerpiece of middle-class homes.
  4. Modern Piano:
    • Today there are two main types: Grand Piano (horizontal strings) and Upright Piano (vertical strings). Digital pianos and keyboards have also made the instrument more portable and accessible.

Fun Fact!

The modern piano has over 10,000 parts, with about 12,000 for a concert grand piano!

How to Find Notes on the Piano Perfectly Every Time

Source: Piano Lessons On The Web

The video emphasizes that a key step in learning to play the piano is knowing how to locate notes. It highlights that the piano keyboard has a repeating pattern of two and three black keys.

The recommended method for finding notes is based on their position relative to these black key groups. For example, C is always found just before a group of two black keys. D is located between the two black keys, and E is to the right of the two black keys. Similarly, F is to the left of a group of three black keys, G and A are within the group of three, and B is to the right of the three black keys.

The video also explains that the musical alphabet goes from A to G and then repeats. Viewers are encouraged to practice finding all instances of notes like C or F across the keyboard. The teacher suggests that while memorizing locations is recommended, counting up or down from known notes like C or F can also be helpful. The ability to quickly find notes improves with practice.


Whole and Half Steps on the Piano

Source: Music Discoveries Piano

This resource explains the foundational musical concepts of semitones (half steps) and whole tones (whole steps) on the piano. A semitone is defined as the shortest distance between any two keys, which can be white-to-black, black-to-white, or white-to-white. The resource clarifies the difference between a chromatic semitone, where the two notes share the same letter name (like D to D sharp), and a diatonic semitone, where they have different letter names (like D to E flat).

A whole tone is explained as being equal to two semitones and can also occur between various key combinations. The resource includes practice examples to help viewers identify these intervals on the keyboard and correctly name the resulting notes. Understanding these concepts is key to navigating the piano keyboard and understanding musical relationships.


Accidentals for Beginners

Source: The Mento Zone

This resource introduces fundamental musical symbols called accidentals: the flat, sharp, and natural signs. These symbols change the pitch of a note. A flat lowers a note by one half step, while a sharp raises it by one half step. A natural sign cancels a previous accidental, returning the note to its original pitch.

A key concept explained is the one-measure rule: an accidental on a specific note affects all subsequent instances of that same note (same letter name) within the same measure. Natural signs are necessary to override this rule within the measure. The resource also discusses enharmonic notes, which are notes that sound the same but have different names (like A sharp and B flat). Importantly, the natural half steps between B and C, and E and F, mean these pairs typically don't have "in-between" notes in the same way.


Accidentals on Piano

Source: PianoTips

This resource explains fundamental musical accidentals: the sharp and flat signs. These symbols are used to change the pitch of a note. A sharp raises a note by one semitone, which is defined as the shortest distance between any two adjacent keys on the piano. To find a sharp, you move to the closest key directly to the right. Conversely, a flat lowers a note by one semitone, meaning you move to the closest key to the left. The resource demonstrates finding these modified notes, including instances where white keys without an adjacent black key are affected, such as E sharp being the same as F, and B sharp being the same as C. Similarly, C flat is the same as B, and F flat is the same as E. The resource also highlights that black keys often have two names, serving as both a sharp of the note below and a flat of the note above (e.g., F sharp is the same note as G flat). Understanding accidentals is essential for playing music that utilizes the black keys and goes beyond basic white-key melodies.


How to Play Major Scales on Piano

Source: Pianote

This resource provides a simple method for playing any major scale on the piano using a specific formula based on the distance between notes, measured in whole steps and half steps. A half step is the shortest distance between any two adjacent keys. A whole step is equal to two half steps.

The formula for any major scale is: Whole step, whole step, half step, whole step, whole step, whole step, half step. Starting on any note, you move up the keyboard following this pattern of whole and half steps. For example, a C major scale uses only white keys (C-D-E-F-G-A-B-C), fitting the pattern. A G major scale requires an F sharp to fit the formula, and an E major scale requires F sharp, G sharp, C sharp, and D sharp. Understanding this formula allows you to construct major scales starting from any key.

Minor Scales on Piano

Source: Margaret Denton

Minor scales are more complex than major scales because they exist in three different forms. It's noted that it's worthwhile to learn all three fluently. These forms are the Natural Minor, the Harmonic Minor, and the Melodic Minor. The Natural Minor scale has no alterations and uses the exact same notes as its relative major scale. The Harmonic Minor scale is created by raising the 7th scale degree when played both ascending and descending. The Melodic Minor scale raises the 6th and 7th scale degrees when ascending, but these degrees are lowered back to the natural minor form when descending. This means that the descending melodic minor scale is the same as the natural minor scale.

The source also distinguishes between different types of relationships minor scales have with major scales. Relative Minor scales are those that share the same key signature as their relative major scale, and they are built on the 6th scale degree of that major scale. A quick method to find a major key's relative minor is to count down 3 half-steps from the tonic note of the major scale. For example, C Major and A Minor are relative scales. In contrast, Parallel Minor scales are built on the same tonic note as a major scale but importantly do not share the same key signature. C Major and C Minor are examples of parallel scales. The source lists various minor scales, indicating they can start on white keys (like C, D, E, F, G, A, B Minor) and black keys (like C#, Eb, F#, G#, Bb Minor).
