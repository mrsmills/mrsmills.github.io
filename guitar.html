The Guitar: History & Types

A Brief History

The guitar is a fretted musical instrument that typically has six strings. It is held flat against the player's body and played by strumming or plucking the strings with the dominant hand, while simultaneously pressing selected strings against frets with the fingers of the opposite hand.

The modern guitar originated in Spain in the 16th century, evolving from earlier stringed instruments like the lute and vihuela. By the 19th century, Antonio de Torres Jurado developed the modern classical guitar shape. In the 20th century, the electric guitar was invented, revolutionizing popular music.

Types of Guitars

Guitars come in various forms, each with unique characteristics:

  1. Acoustic Guitar
    • Hollow body that amplifies the strings' vibration
    • Steel strings produce bright, loud sound
    • Common in folk, country, and pop music
  2. Classical Guitar
    • Nylon strings for softer, mellower tone
    • Wider neck for fingerstyle playing
    • Used in classical and flamenco music
  3. Electric Guitar
    • Solid or semi-hollow body requires amplification
    • Magnetic pickups convert string vibrations to electrical signals
    • Versatile sound shaped by amplifiers and effects
  4. Bass Guitar
    • Lower-pitched instrument with thicker strings
    • Typically has four strings (E-A-D-G)
    • Provides rhythmic and harmonic foundation

Fun Fact!

The world's oldest known guitar-like instrument is over 3,500 years old! It was a tanbur from Egypt, belonging to the singer Har-Mose.

How to Read Tablature on Guitar

Source: Andrew McKenzie

This resource introduces Understanding Tablature (Tab), explaining it as an easy method for reading music and knowing where to place your fingers on the guitar. Tablature is depicted as having six horizontal lines, with each line representing one of the guitar's six strings. A key point is that the top line of the tab corresponds to the bottom, thinnest string (string #1) on your guitar, while the bottom line corresponds to the top, thickest string (string #6). Numbers found on these lines tell you which fret to press down on that specific string. A zero (0) indicates that you should play the string open without pressing any fret. How to Read a Guitar Chord Chart and Play D Major Chord

Source: JustinGuitar

This video explains how to read a chord diagram, which is very helpful for guitarists. A chord diagram uses six vertical lines to represent the guitar's six strings, from thickest to thinnest. The horizontal lines show the frets, with the nut sometimes marked at the top. Dots on the diagram indicate where to put your fingers. Often, numbers near the dots specify which finger to use (1=index, 2=middle, 3=ring, 4=pinky). An X above a string means you should not play that string. A zero (0) means you play that string open, without pressing any frets. Understanding these elements helps you learn finger placement for different chords.

How to Tune Guitar with a Digital Tuner

Source: Austin Bazaar

This video resource teaches you how to tune your guitar using a digital chromatic tuner. It's suitable for electric, acoustic, and nylon-string guitars. The video explains that the standard notes for the guitar strings are E, A, D, G, B, and a higher E. The digital tuner helps you know if a string is in tune. If the tuner's needle is in the middle, it's in tune. If it's to the left, the string is "flat" (too low), and you need to tighten it by turning the machine head counter-clockwise. If the needle is to the right, the string is "sharp" (too high), and you should loosen the tension. The aim is to adjust the string until the tuner shows it's in tune, often turning green. The video demonstrates tuning each string using this process and suggests checking the tuning by ear afterward.

A Brief History of the Guitar

Source: Paul Guy

The guitar is an ancient instrument with a history spanning over 4000 years. Its ancestors include early stringed instruments like bowl harps and tanburs found in ancient civilizations. Research shows the guitar did not evolve from the lute or the Greek kithara; they shared common ancestors but followed separate paths. The name "guitar" is believed to come from the ancient Persian word "chartar," meaning "four strings," which described an early related instrument. This four-stringed instrument evolved in Spain, eventually gaining a fifth, then a sixth "course" (pair of strings), before transitioning to six single strings like the modern guitar. The design of the modern classical guitar was largely standardized in the mid-19th century, followed later by the development of steel-string acoustics and electric guitars.
